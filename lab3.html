Gravimetric Analysis

Jan MacGregor - Oct. 20th, 2020

Purpose

  In this lab, a container with a partially damaged label was given, which read that the 500g of contained salt had the formula XCl2 (X being the part scratched out, it was two characters), indicating that the element X was an alkaline earth metal. The intent of the following lab was to determine the unknown element, and thus the full formula of the salt, using gravimetric analysis.

Background Info

  There is an direct relationship between the mass of a substance and the number of atoms in that mass, but it is dependent on the substance, more specifically the substance's "molar mass". This, along with the fact that when reacting a substance with another, the resulting molecules have different molar masses than when they began, means that with careful weighing, some algebra, and examination of the possible chemical equations, the identity of a substance can be determined with high certainty, which is what was done in this lab. Some preliminary research was done, and it was determined that two of the possible elements for X, namely radium and strontium, were extremely unlikely candidates, given that RaCl2 is extremely radioactive, and SrCl2 is extremely expensive, a mere 25g was priced at $315 at the time of writing, which would make 500g an absurd amount to own in our lab.

Procedure

  • In order to find a relationship between the mass of the unknown substance and the molar mass (which would be used to identify it), the unknown was mixed with silver nitrate, which, when mixed with any chloride salt in solution, would precipitate out silver chloride. The reaction would be as follows (regardless of which alkaline metal X is, as they all have the same charge):

    XCl2 (aq) + 2AgNO3 (aq) → X(NO3)2 (aq) + 2AgCl (s)
  • 3.0456g of XCl2 was measured out, and stirred into 50ml of demineralized water. Demineralized water had to be used in order to preserve the accuracy of this analysis, by preventing any other precipitates from forming and raising our yield.
  • In order to preserve accuracy, it had to be ensured that regardless of what element X was, there would be enough silver to fully react with the weighed salt. Since the number of moles per gram decreases as the weight of an atom increases, the "lightest" possibility for X must be used when calculating the required silver, in order to ensure that the silver is in excess, and the lightest alkaline metal is Be, at 9.012 amu. Using beryllium, BeCl2 weighs 79.918 amu, and the number of moles of BeCl2 weighed was:

    3.0456g ÷ 79.918g/mol = 0.038109mol BeCl2
  • Given the ratio of XCl2 to AgNO3 is 1:2, more than twice as many moles of AgNO3 would be required for the silver to be in excess, or more than about 0.076mol of AgNO3. As the available AgNO3 was in the form of a 1M solution, this was made simple by just using 100mL of the solution in the reaction with the XCl2.
  • Once the solutions were mixed, a cloudy white precipitate formed (the silver chloride), which was poured off into a vaccuum filter, and the pump turned on.
  • While the vaccuum filter was going, a watchglass was tared on the scale, with nothing on the watchglass and the door to the scale closed. It was left in the scale until needed.
  • After the precipitate had dried into a cake, it was placed on the watchglass that was in the scale, which was then placed into the drying oven for 30 minutes, along with dessicant pellets.
  • After 30 minutes, the dried precipitate on the watchglass was placed back on the scale, and came out to 8.5341g of silver chloride.

Conclusion

  Given a greater than 90% yield, which is reasonable due to the degree of care given to this lab, the unknown was most likely Mg. This means the reaction was

MgCl2 (aq) + 2AgNO3 (aq) → Mg(NO3)2 (aq) + 2AgCl (s)

and the percent yield was

(8.5341g ÷ 9.1399g) * 100 = 93.4% yield

Experimental Sources of Error

  One thing that could have lowered the yield was that when the flask of reacted MgCl2 and AgNO3 was poured into the vaccuum filter, no water was sprayed in to wash out the remaining product. Some of the MgCl2 might have stuck to the filter paper, which would've also lowered the yield. The purity of the MgCl2 was shown, and it was quite high, so this was not factored into the calculations, but it would have lowered the yield. Had not all of the MgCl2 reacted, the yield would've been much lower, but given that the silver was in excess, this is not a likely source of error.
