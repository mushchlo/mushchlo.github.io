Standard Deviation Lab

Jan MacGregor, 10/4/2020

Purpose

The purpose of this lab was to identify an unknown ion in solution, using qualitative analysis.

Procedure

  • Using the flowchart below, the solution of unknown ions was first poured into the test tube, then 2 drops of 3M HCl was added to the solution, creating a white cloudy precipatate. The tube was centrifuged to drop the precipatate out of suspension, and the remaining liquid was decanted into another test tube for later analysis.
  • 2 drops of deionized water were added to the precipatate, and the test tube containing the unknown precipatate was placed in a heat bath of boiling water, to dissolve any slightly soluble ions, then quickly placed in the centrifuge and decanted to remove the slightly soluble precipatate, which dropped out once placed in a separate test tube.
  • The separated slightly soluble ion, thought to be Pb2+, was added to a solution of Na2CrO4 which, in the case of a bright yellow precipatate, would confirm the existence of Pb2+ ions. It did produce a yellow precipatate, so Pb2+ was one of the ions contained in the unknown solution
  • To the first test tube, with the insoluble (and still unknown) precipatate, NH3 was added dropwise until it turned red litmus blue (until basic), then centrifuged to drop the now brown precipatate out of suspension, and decanted once more, into a fourth test tube.
  • The decanted solution was then tested for Ag+ ions by adding HCl until the solution turned blue litmus red, which, in the case of white, cloudy precipatate, would've confirmed Ag+. However, no precipatate was produced, so the unknown did not contain any Ag+ ions.
  • To the remaining brown precipatate in the original test tube, Na2CrO4 was added, which in the case of a bright orange precipatate, would confirm the presence of Hg22+. A bright orange precipatate was formed, and thus the unknown did contain Hg22+.

Conclusion

Thus, the unknown solution of ions contained Pb2+ and Hg22+
